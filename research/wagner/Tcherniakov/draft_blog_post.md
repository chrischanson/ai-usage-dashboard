# The Tcherniakov Problem: When *Regietheater* Ruins Wagner — and When It Doesn't

*Over two decades and sixteen major productions, Dmitri Tcherniakov has made himself indispensable and infuriating in equal measure.*

---

Anyone who makes the pilgrimage to the Bayreuth Festival or settles into the velvet seats of the Berlin Staatsoper arrives with a certain expectation. Richard Wagner did not write mere plays. He wrote cosmic, spiritual epics that were designed, in his own words, to consecrate the stage. His operas have their own symbolic grammar — the Rhine, the Grail, the World Ash Tree, the magic fire — and that grammar is inseparable from the experience of hearing the music.

Enter Dmitri Tcherniakov.

The Russian director, born in Moscow in 1970, has spent the last two decades systematically dismantling that grammar. Across an extraordinary body of work — from his career-making *Eugene Onegin* at the Bolshoi in 2006 to his *Der Ring des Nibelungen* at the Berlin Staatsoper in 2022 — he has become the modern poster child for uncompromising *Regietheater*, the director's theater tradition that places a provocateur's conceptual vision above fidelity to the composer's original intentions.

The results have been, depending on your perspective, either revelatory or catastrophic. Often both.

---

The tool, in each case, is the same: strip away the supernatural and mythological apparatus, transplant the action into a hyper-realistic institutional or domestic setting, and focus obsessively on close-quarters psychological truth. In the right opera, this is electrifying. In the wrong one, it is a wrecking ball.

---

## When the Wrecking Ball Strikes: Wagner's Sacred Epics

Tcherniakov's 2015 *Parsifal* — conducted by Daniel Barenboim at the Berlin Staatsoper — is the sharpest example of his method at its most destructive. Instead of an order of holy knights guarding the Grail in Montsalvat's forest, we are given a crumbling, institutional wooden hall. The knights are not noble warriors; they are traumatized, codependent, aging men in winter coats and beanies, trapped in a cycle of grief, obsessing over Amfortas's unhealing wound as if it were their collective therapy session. The Grail ritual becomes a toxic performance of suffering, with Gurnemanz cast as a rigid sectarian elder keeping his flock compliant rather than a wise mentor guiding a spiritual heir.

Act II turns Klingsor's enchanted garden into a clinical psychology lab, with the Flowermaidens reimagined as captive adolescents in brightly colored clothes. Kundry, rather than a shapeshifting enchantress, is weaponized by Klingsor to exploit Parsifal's childhood trauma — the death and abandonment of his mother, Herzeleide — in a seduction scene that plays out in a terrifyingly domestic interior.

The final act's subversion is Tcherniakov at his most ruthless. The "Good Friday Spell" — traditionally Wagner's most radiant passage of spiritual rebirth, nature itself rejoicing at Parsifal's return — takes place in the same bleak hall, unchanged. And then comes the ending: Parsifal heals Amfortas, only for the knights to immediately attempt to crown him as their new idol. He rejects them. Gurnemanz, desperate to preserve the cult's structure, stabs Kundry dead. The opera closes not with transcendence but with Parsifal cradling her corpse, the knights singing "Redemption to the Redeemer" with blind, unseeing fanaticism in the background.

I understand precisely what Tcherniakov is doing. The production is, intellectually, a devastatingly coherent critique of how dogma destroys human love. But Wagner did not write *Parsifal* as a critique. He wrote it as a *Bühnenweihfestspiel* — a festival play for the consecration of the stage. It was meant to invoke the sublime, the spiritual, the sense of redemption that transcends human categories. When you replace that with a grim psychological case study, you have not interpreted the work; you have overwritten it.

The only reason the Berlin *Parsifal* registers at all as a profound experience is Barenboim's conducting. His tempos are expansive, deeply felt, heavy with sorrow. While Tcherniakov shows you the clinical, ugly reality of human trauma on stage, Barenboim's orchestra expresses the profound, aching inner life of these characters. The music provides the soul that the staging deliberately withholds. The two are in active conflict, and the music is doing all the work.

His 2022 *Ring* at the same house amplified the problem to epic scale. The entire four-opera cycle — fifteen hours of music — was set inside the **E.S.C.H.E.** institute, a fictional multi-level 1970s scientific research complex. The name is a Tcherniakov pun: E.S.C.H.E. stands for *Experimental Scientific Centre for Human Evolution*, but *Esche* in German also means ash tree — a sly reference to the World Ash Tree (*Weltesche*), the mythic source of Wotan's spear and power. It is exactly the kind of clever literary detail that makes Tcherniakov's defenders so enthusiastic about his work. And yet: when the characters are singing about that tree, about the Rhine, about the wild forests, about the elemental forces of nature in conflict — you are looking at laboratory rooms, sensory deprivation chambers, corporate boardrooms, and fluorescent lighting.

There are no Rhinemaidens swimming. There is no magic fire. There is no forest for Siegfried to wander in. The vast, elemental world of Wagner's imagination has been compressed into four walls and a revolving stage structure. The cycle drew a barrage of audience boos at its conclusion.

Barenboim, tragically, had to withdraw from conducting due to severe health issues, and Christian Thielemann stepped in. The artistic collision was extraordinary in its own way: Tcherniakov's hyper-modern clinical staging colliding with Thielemann's deeply traditional, monumental, late-Romantic musical reading. Once again, the music carried the drama the production refused to serve.

The same diagnosis applies to the 2018 *Tristan und Isolde*. Here the lovers were confined to a luxury yacht in Act I and a sterile apartment in Act II. Isolde's journey from furious noblewoman to transcendent lover — the very arc that Schopenhauer's philosophy of Will and resignation shaped Wagner's conception of — was recast as a desperate rebellion against a cruel wealthy elite. Intellectually rigorous. Phenomenally sung. But grounding a fundamentally metaphysical opera in the mundane realities of middle-class morality is a fundamental misunderstanding of what the music is asking the stage to be.

## When the Clinic Works: The Case of *Der fliegende Holländer*

With that track record, I should have written off Tcherniakov's 2021 Bayreuth debut — his first time on the Green Hill — as another disappointment. Instead, his *Der fliegende Holländer* is the production that made me genuinely rethink my position.

Surprisingly: I don't dislike it at all. In fact, it works brilliantly.

The reason becomes obvious when you ask the simple question Tcherniakov's critics often forget to ask: *what kind of opera is this, actually?* Unlike *Parsifal* or the *Ring*, *The Flying Dutchman* is not a cosmic philosophical epic about gods, fate, and the transcendence of human love. It is a psychological thriller. It is a story about isolation, obsession, small-town cruelty, and the cyclical transmission of trauma. Wagner was twenty-eight when he wrote it, drawing on his own stormy sea voyage, and the opera has an urgent, compact, almost cinematic energy that his later works deliberately abandoned in favor of transcendence.

In Tcherniakov's staging, the Dutchman is not a supernatural sea-captain under a mythic curse. He is a man who was exiled from a repressive, xenophobic provincial town after the community's cruelty drove his mother to suicide. He returns, decades later, with a briefcase full of money and a plan for calculated revenge. When Daland sells him his daughter's hand — Daland here reimagined as the town's prominent, complacent civic patriarch — the transaction exposes the cheap, transactional morality of the community with surgical precision.

Senta is not a delusional girl singing to a painting of a ghost. She is the only genuine rebel in the town — the one person who recognizes the Dutchman's rage as a mirror of her own. When she sings her famous Ballad in Act II, she is performing a deliberate, theatrical provocation, mocking the town's own mythologies in front of the uncomfortable bourgeois women around her.

And the ending: rather than throwing herself into the sea, Senta's chaperone Mary steps out of the crowd with a shotgun and shoots the Dutchman dead. Senta walks away laughing — using the chaos to escape her stifling life.

This is not an act of violence against Wagner's text. This is an act of interpretive intelligence that matches the material. The scope of *Holländer* is already domestic and claustrophobic. The claustrophobia is the opera's subject. Tcherniakov did not need to shrink anything; the world of the opera was already the size of a boardroom.

The production was a musical triumph as well. Oksana Lyniv made history as the first woman to conduct at Bayreuth, and Asmik Grigorian's Senta was a sensation — febrile, vocally commanding, psychologically completely inhabited. The staging and the music were not at war. They were telling the same story.

## The One Rule Tcherniakov Cannot Escape

What separates the *Holländer* from the *Ring* is not the quality of the conceptual thinking — in both cases, the thinking is sophisticated and internally consistent. The difference is whether the concept works *with* the music's fundamental nature or *against* it.

Wagner's later operas — the *Ring*, *Parsifal*, *Tristan* — are metaphysical by design. Their music reaches toward something beyond the human. That is not a staging tradition; it is baked into the harmonic language, the leitmotif architecture, the structural pacing. When Tcherniakov places that music inside a 1970s research institute or aboard a luxury yacht, he creates an irreconcilable conflict: the music is pointing upward and outward, and the staging is pointing inward and down.

*Holländer* does not reach for the transcendent in the same way. It reaches for the raw, the human, the pathologically specific. Tcherniakov is very, very good at the raw and pathologically specific.

There is a lesson here — one that Tcherniakov himself seems unwilling to learn. His analytical intelligence is formidable. His ability to direct singers at close quarters, to make familiar characters feel startlingly real, to expose the institutional hypocrisies inside a dramatic situation — these are genuine gifts. Applied to the right material, he produces work that is genuinely revelatory: the *Tsar Saltan* at La Monnaie in 2019, reframed as the story of a mother and an autistic son and awarded Best New Production at the International Opera Awards, is evidence that his empathy can be as powerful as his cynicism.

But applied to Wagner's great sacred epics, the method is simply the wrong tool. And the boos that greeted his *Ring* in Berlin — like those that rang out at La Scala for his *Traviata* in 2013, or at Aix for his *Carmen* in 2017 — are not the reactions of audiences who failed to understand him. They are the reactions of audiences who understood him perfectly and found his answer unsatisfying.

Wagner's grandest works demand a sense of the sublime. No amount of brilliant stage-craft — no clever acronym, no revolving lab set, no psychologically devastated cult — can substitute for it.

---

*For those seeking an antidote: Hans Knappertsbusch's 1962 Bayreuth recording of Parsifal remains the irreplaceable audio benchmark. For visual productions, Otto Schenk's 1992 Met staging (cond. Levine) and Hans-Jürgen Syberberg's 1982 film (cond. Jordan) offer vastly different but equally sincere engagements with the mythic grandeur Tcherniakov refuses.*

| **Tristan und Isolde** *(Wagner)* [1, 3] | 2005 / 2018 | Mariinsky Theatre / Berlin Staatsoper | In 2018, the lovers are confined to a luxury yacht in Act I and a sterile apartment in Act II, removing the metaphysical transcendence of night and death. | **Polarizing.** Strongly criticized for bringing a cosmic, philosophical work down to the level of mundane domestic drama, though musical values (under Barenboim) were praised. |

Damrau universally praised; direction and Gatti were the primary targets of protest. Press split between "travesty" and psychological depth. |
| **Don Giovanni** *(Mozart)* [6, 7] | 2010 | Festival d'Aix-en-Provence | Set inside the Commendatore’s book-lined dining room/library. Re-imagined as a dysfunctional bourgeois family drama; the Don is a lonely, aging disruptor. | **Divisive.** Detractors called the staging bizarre and disconnected from the text, while supporters praised the intense psychological interactions between characters. |
| **Prince Igor** *(Borodin)* [8, 9] | 2014 | Metropolitan Opera (New York) | Anti-militaristic, multimedia psycho-drama highlighting Igor's inner war trauma. Incorporated Borodin's lost sketches while stripping away later editorial changes. | **Highly Praised.** Lauded as a musical and theatrical revelation, although some found the multimedia projections distracting. |
| **Parsifal** *(Wagner)* [1, 3] | 2015 | Staatsoper Unter den Linden (Berlin) | Montsalvat is a crumbling wooden-hall cult of traumatized, codependent men. Klingsor's garden is a clinical lab of captive girls. Kundry has agency and is killed by Gurnemanz. | **Highly Polarized.** Received a barrage of audience boos for the ending. Critics praised the extraordinary singing-acting and Daniel Barenboim’s incandescent pacing. |
| **Lulu** *(Berg)* [10] | 2015 | Bayerische Staatsoper (Munich) | Staged in a clinical, wire-mesh, abstract space focusing on raw human desire and isolation. | **Highly Acclaimed.** Praised for psychological intensity and Marlis Petersen's performance. |
| **Iolanta / The Nutcracker** *(Tchaikovsky)* [11] | 2016 | Opéra national de Paris | A double bill where Iolanta’s cure from blindness transitions directly into Marie’s psychological, dark dreamscape in the ballet. | **Mixed.** Praised for conceptual connections, but criticized for the melancholy tone of the ballet. |
| **Carmen** *(Bizet)* [12] | 2017 | Festival d'Aix-en-Provence | Framed as a radical group-therapy/role-playing session inside a modern psychiatric facility. | **Highly Divisive.** Critics felt the clinical frame stripped away the raw passion, though some admired its intellectual framework. |
| **Tsar Saltan** *(Rimsky-Korsakov)* [10] | 2019 | La Monnaie (Brussels) | Re-imagined as a story of a mother and her autistic son, using animation to show the child's inner coping mechanisms. | **Universally Acclaimed.** Won "Best New Production" at the 2020 International Opera Awards; praised for emotional warmth. |
| **Elektra** *(Strauss)* [13] | 2019 / 2021 | Staatsoper Hamburg | Set in a modern, claustrophobic bourgeois apartment, stripping away Greek myth for clinical family pathology. | **Acclaimed.** Hailed as a gripping psycho-thriller with precise, cinematic character direction. |
| **Der fliegende Holländer** *(Wagner)* [14, 15] | 2021 | Bayreuth Festival | Set in a modern port town; the Dutchman is an outcast seeking calculated revenge for his mother's suicide. Mary stabs/shoots the Dutchman. | **Divisive Staging, Musical Triumph.** Praised for Oksana Lyniv's historic conducting and Asmik Grigorian's Senta. |
| **Der Ring des Nibelungen** *(Wagner)* [16, 17] | 2022 | Staatsoper Unter den Linden (Berlin) | Set in the **E.S.C.H.E.** (*Experimental Scientific Centre for Human Evolution*) institute — a deliberate pun on *Esche* (German: ash tree), referencing Wotan's World Ash Tree. Gods are scientists; Valhalla is a corporate lab. | **Highly Divisive.** Drew widespread boos at the end for stripping nature and myth, though praised for intellectual consistency and Thielemann's conducting. |
