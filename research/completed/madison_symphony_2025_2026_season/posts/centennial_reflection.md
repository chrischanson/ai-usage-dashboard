# A Century of Sound: Personal Reflections on the MSO Centennial Season

*From the front row under the podium to shared family moments, reflecting on the Madison Symphony Orchestra's historic 100th season and John DeMain's final bow.*

---

## Introduction

As the Madison Symphony Orchestra (MSO) draws its historic 100th Centennial Season to a close, Overture Hall has been filled with both high-caliber music and a palpable sense of nostalgia. For me, this milestone is deeply personal. I first started attending MSO concerts back in 2012. Over the last fourteen years, the orchestra (whose official website is [madisonsymphony.org](https://madisonsymphony.org)) has become a central thread in my life in Madison. Watching it grow, seeing its audience evolve, and experiencing the incredible acoustics of Overture Hall has been a constant joy—particularly in how the Overture Center has fulfilled Maestro John DeMain's core vision: opening its doors wide for the entire public to enter and enjoy this music together.

This centennial season was especially significant as it marked the farewell of Music Director John DeMain after 32 years of visionary leadership. Throughout the year, I found myself reflecting on this shared community space and the unique magic of live performance, starting with a night of high-flying acrobatics.

---

## The Circus and the Maestro: Soar with Cirque de la Symphonie

The opening celebration weekend in September 2025 set a festive tone, particularly Saturday night's *Soar with Cirque de la Symphonie*. It was an absolute blast. Watching acrobats, jugglers, and aerialists perform high-wire and silk acts suspended directly above the orchestra was a visual marvel, but the real highlight for me was seeing the fun, lighthearted side of Maestro John DeMain. 

Normally a figure of intense classical focus, DeMain conducted the film scores of John Williams and Hans Zimmer with a visible spark of playfulness, smiling and cueing the orchestra in perfect synchronization with the physical feats above. It was a wonderful reminder of DeMain's versatility and his commitment to making the symphony an inclusive, joyful space for the entire Madison community.

---

## Under the Podium: An Intimate Mahler 2 for Primal Light

For the subscription opener in October, *Primal Light*, I decided to treat myself to a unique experience: I bought a ticket in the very first row. Sitting directly under the conductor's podium, I was so close I could hear the rustle of DeMain's baton cutting through the air and watch the intense, non-verbal communication between him and the principal players. 

Performing Gustav Mahler’s Symphony No. 2 ("Resurrection") from this vantage point was overwhelming. The sheer physical wave of sound from the strings, the brass, and the Madison Symphony Chorus felt as if it were washing right through me. I didn't just listen to the music—I felt the vibrations in the floorboards. For those two hours, I wasn't just an audience member; I felt like a physical part of the orchestra itself. When the chorus and organ reached their glorious climax in the final movement, the experience was transcendent.

---

## Re-creating the Magic: Radiance and the Cello

Chasing that same raw, immersive feeling, I wanted to share it with my family. When the *Radiance* concert came around in November—featuring cellist Alban Gerhardt and Mussorgsky's *Pictures at an Exhibition*—I bought tickets for us in that same front-row section. 

However, live music has a way of being unpredictable, and the experience wasn't quite the same. Sitting that close as a group can feel a bit overwhelming, and without the massive choral and brass architecture of Mahler's Second, the sonic balance in the absolute front row can be slightly askew for classical concertos. 

That said, the concert was still spectacular. Guest conductor Robert Moody led the orchestra with a vibrant, colorful energy, and cellist Alban Gerhardt was absolutely outstanding. His performance of Haydn’s Cello Concerto No. 2 was a masterclass in elegance, warmth, and precision. Even if the seating arrangement didn't quite capture the lightning-in-a-bottle magic of the Mahler concert, sharing Gerhardt's virtuosity with my family made it a cherished memory.

---

## A Showcase of Tomorrow: The Final Forte

Over the last several years, another annual highlight for us has been attending *The Final Forte* (the final round of the Bolz Young Artist Competition). It is always incredibly inspiring to see such a diverse variety of gifted young Wisconsin musicians performing at a professional level across different disciplines. Watching these high school finalists perform concerto movements on violin, piano, or marimba with the full orchestra under Maestro DeMain's supportive baton is a powerful testament to the musical future of our state.

For the 20th anniversary broadcast on March 4, 2026, the competition was particularly fierce. It ended with pianist **André Peck** (performing the first movement of Prokofiev's Piano Concerto No. 1) winning first place and the prestigious **Marian Bolz Prize for Distinguished Musical Achievement**. The other three finalists—violinists **Lorenz Fradkin-Annen** and **Cordelia Momo Fredrickson**, and marimbist **Zachary Lemke**—also played beautifully, earning the **Steenbock Youth Music Award** and **Honorable Mentions** for their outstanding performances.

---

## Bruckner, Bernstein, and Linz Echoes: Voices Eternal

In May, the subscription finale, *Voices Eternal*, offered an exceptionally moving and personal experience. Hearing Leonard Bernstein’s *Chichester Psalms* live was fascinating, with its intricate syncopated rhythms and intense vocal demands. However, it was my first time experiencing Anton Bruckner’s monumental *Te Deum* live that truly took my breath away. 

As the triumphant choral and brass melodies filled Overture Hall, my mind immediately drifted back to a trip I took last year (2025) to Linz, Austria. I had visited the historic St. Florian Monastery, the very abbey where Bruckner worked as an organist for many years and where he now lies buried directly beneath the great St. Florian organ. Hearing his sacred masterpiece live bridged my travels with this hometown performance, making the music feel intensely alive. It was a perfect, transcendent climax for John DeMain's final subscription concert series.

---

## The Grand Finale: For the Love of Music

Tonight, June 14, 2026, I attended the absolute final performance conducted by John DeMain as Music Director: the *For the Love of Music* Centennial Festival Symphony Concert. Offered completely free to the community, Overture Hall was packed to the gills, embodying the very idea Maestro DeMain has championed from the stage throughout his career—that the Overture Center must open its doors wide for the public to enter and enjoy music without barriers. 

Opening with George Gershwin’s *An American in Paris*, the concert had an electric, celebratory feel. Julian Rhee’s performance of Sarasate’s *Carmen Fantasy* was absolutely sensational. The crowd's thunderous, roaring applause coaxed him back for a rare and thrilling treat: an encore of Sarasate's dramatic and fiery *Zigeunerweisen* performed not as a solo, but **with the full orchestra**. Because orchestral encores require coordination and rehearsal, this surprise performance felt like a specially prepared gift to Maestro John DeMain on his final night. 

In the second half, the orchestra performed Sibelius' majestic *Finlandia*, which was guest-conducted with great energy by local favorite **Lori Grapentine**. This was followed by DeMain conducting *Mars*, *Venus*, *Mercury*, and *Jupiter* from Gustav Holst’s *The Planets*, providing a thrilling, cosmic sonic journey. Finally, the choral finale of Beethoven’s Ninth Symphony was a magnificent, unifying moment that resulted in a roaring, ten-minute standing ovation. For me, hearing this monumental work was a deeply emotional full-circle moment. When DeMain completed his very first season as Music Director back in May 1995, he concluded it by conducting Beethoven’s Ninth Symphony at the old Oscar Mayer Theatre. Bookending his historic 32-year tenure with the exact same masterwork in Overture Hall felt like a transcendent tribute to the journey we have all shared with him.

But the magic didn't end when the curtain fell. The celebration spilled out into the Overture Center lobby for a free public reception. Raising a glass of Prosecco for a community toast to Maestro DeMain, enjoying the dessert bar, and listening to the local band **Grupo Candela** play live Afro-Cuban and Latin jazz was the perfect end to an unforgettable evening and an era.

## Beyond Overture Hall: Symphony at Sunset and Madison’s Outdoor Traditions

While Overture Hall is the primary home of the Madison Symphony Orchestra, the MSO has a rich history of engaging the community in outdoor spaces. One of my favorite summer traditions is the MSO League's annual **Symphony at Sunset** (formerly *Concert on the Green*), held at the beautiful **Burrows Park** on the eastern shore of Lake Mendota. It’s a wonderful experience—sipping wine and listening to MSO chamber ensembles perform as the sun sets over the water. Due to the sheer scale of the centennial festival celebrations this June, the sunset concert took a brief hiatus for 2026, but it is set to return in 2027.

Writing about outdoor classical music in Madison always brings up a funny, near-universal local confusion: **Concerts on the Square**. Almost every Madisonian has spent a Wednesday evening on the Capitol lawn eating cheese curds and listening to the orchestra, but many don't realize that this beloved series is actually produced and performed by the **Wisconsin Chamber Orchestra (WCO)**, a completely separate organization from the MSO! Both groups share Overture Hall for their indoor classical programs, but their outdoor summer identities are distinct, creating a vibrant, dual-orchestra culture that makes Madison such an incredible place for live music.

## Conclusion: Farewell to a Legend

Looking back at these moments—from the playful cinema scores of the Cirque weekend to the monumental heights of the front-row Mahler, the inspiring youth showcases, the deep echoes of Bruckner, and tonight's joyous lobby celebration—the MSO's 100th season has been a fitting tribute to a century of music-making and DeMain's incredible legacy. As the MSO moves into its next century under new leadership, the foundation built by John DeMain ensures that Madison's love affair with the symphony will endure for generations to come.
