# 🌅 Primal Light

**Dates:** October 17–19, 2025 (Subscription Concert 1)  
**Conductor:** John DeMain  
**Featured Soloists:**  
*   **Christopher Taylor** (Piano)  
*   **Jeni Houser** (Soprano)  
*   **Emily Fons** (Mezzo-Soprano)  
*   **Madison Symphony Chorus** (Beverly Taylor, Director)  
**Venue:** Overture Hall, Madison, WI  

---

## 📖 Concert Background & Context
"Primal Light" served as the official subscription season opener for the Madison Symphony Orchestra’s historic 100th anniversary season. The title of the program is the English translation of "Urlicht," the name of the spiritual fourth movement of Gustav Mahler’s massive Symphony No. 2 ("Resurrection"). Music Director John DeMain chose Mahler’s monumental symphony as a profound artistic statement to launch his 32nd and final season with the orchestra, representing themes of death, rebirth, and celebration.

To balance the massive Mahler symphony, the first half of the concert featured two contrasting works: the Madison premiere of Mason Bates' brief, celebratory *Resurrexit* (composed in 2024 to herald a rebirth of live music), and César Franck’s romantic *Symphonic Variations*, featuring internationally renowned pianist and UW–Madison faculty member Christopher Taylor.

## ✍️ Critical Review & Audience Reception
Local writeups and reviews from classical music columnists in Madison (including coverage in *The Cap Times* and classical reviews) hailed the concert as a landmark achievement:

*   **Franck’s Symphonic Variations:** Pianist Christopher Taylor was praised for his "sparkling virtuosity" and intellectual depth. Reviewers noted that his phrasing was fluid and organic, engaging in a lively, chamber-like dialogue with the orchestra rather than fighting to overpower it.
*   **Mahler’s "Resurrection" Symphony:** The performance of Mahler's Second was described as an emotional, tour-de-force reading by John DeMain. 
    *   The brass section was highly commended for its power, precision, and the staging of the offstage brass calls in the final movement, which created a haunting, three-dimensional acoustic effect in Overture Hall.
    *   Mezzo-soprano Emily Fons’ delivery of the "Urlicht" movement was praised as a spiritual highlight, delivered with deep, resonant, and unaffected simplicity that captured the "innocent faith" Mahler intended.
    *   Soprano Jeni Houser provided a soaring, clear tone that blended beautifully with the chorus.
    *   The Madison Symphony Chorus, trained by Beverly Taylor, was praised for its initial hushed, whispered entry (*"Aufersteh'n"*) and its eventual build-up to a thunderous, triumphant wall of sound.
*   **The Verdict:** Critics described this concert as one of the most powerful and technically polished performances in the MSO's history. The final chords, augmented by the Overture Concert Organ, left the audience in stunned silence before erupting into a long, emotional standing ovation.
